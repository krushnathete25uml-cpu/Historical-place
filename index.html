What is Raigad Fort?

Raigad Fort, locally called Rairi, is one of the most majestic and historically significant hill forts in India. Perched atop a sheer basalt plateau in the Sahyadri mountain range of Maharashtra, it rises nearly 820 metres above sea level, commanding a panoramic view of the surrounding valleys. Its towering walls, intricate gateways, and sprawling complex once housed an entire royal court — palaces, markets, temples, barracks, and granaries — making it a self-sufficient mountain capital.

The fort was originally built around 1030 CE by the Chandrarao More clan, but it was Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj who transformed it into an impregnable stronghold and the beating heart of the Maratha Empire. He personally supervised its extensive renovation, turning a regional hill fort into a magnificent royal citadel worthy of an emperor's coronation.

Where is it Located?

Raigad Fort is located in Mahad taluka of Raigad district in Maharashtra, roughly 25 kilometres from Mahad town and about 165 kilometres south of Mumbai. The fort sits atop an isolated flat-topped hill surrounded on all sides by deep gorges, making it naturally almost impossible to breach. Today it can be reached by a thrilling ropeway (udan khatola) or by trekking up the ancient 1,400-step stone staircase carved into the hillside.

The region is part of the lush Western Ghats, a UNESCO World Heritage biodiversity hotspot. The fort's strategic location meant it was virtually invisible from the plains below, giving defenders an enormous advantage over any would-be attackers.

When Was It Built?

The origins of Raigad date back to around 1030 CE under the Chandrarao More dynasty. In 1656 CE, Shivaji Maharaj besieged and captured the fort. Over the next decade, he invested enormous resources to rebuild and fortify Raigad on a grand scale. By 1674 CE, the transformation was complete, and Raigad was chosen as the site for Shivaji Maharaj's historic coronation as Chhatrapati — an event that marked the formal founding of the Maratha Empire.

Shivaji Maharaj passed away at Raigad in April 1680, and his samadhi (memorial) still stands on the fort premises and is venerated by millions.

"The fort that cannot be taken by any enemy — it can only be given away." — Said of Raigad by Maratha military strategists

Why is it Historically Important?

Raigad is far more than a military structure — it is the birthplace of Maratha sovereignty. When Shivaji Maharaj was crowned Chhatrapati here on 6 June 1674, it was a watershed moment in Indian history. For the first time in centuries, a Hindu king established an independent sovereign state on the Deccan plateau, free from Mughal suzerainty.

At its peak, Raigad housed over 300 shops in an organised market, royal palaces, a grand audience hall (Diwan-e-Aam), stables, freshwater tanks, and residences for thousands — all at an altitude of 820 metres. In 1818, the fort fell to the British East India Company, who partially demolished many structures. Today the Archaeological Survey of India maintains the site, and Raigad remains a deeply emotional symbol of Maratha pride.

Interesting Facts

  • The fort has over 1,400 stone steps carved into the hillside leading to the top.
  • A modern ropeway (udan khatola) carries visitors to the summit in minutes.
  • At its peak, Raigad's market had over 300 shops — a full trading city on a hilltop.
  • Shivaji Maharaj's samadhi and a small temple dedicated to him are on the fort.
  • The fort has seven gateways, each designed to slow and trap invading forces.
  • Water for the complex was sourced from natural and man-made tanks on the plateau.
  • The Takmak Tok cliff was used as an execution point — criminals were thrown off it.
  • The fort's walls were described by contemporaries as "touching the sky", so vertical were the cliff faces.
  • Raigad was captured by the Mughals (Aurangzeb) in 1689 and held until the Marathas recaptured it.
  • The coronation of 1674 involved scholars from across India and was performed over several elaborate days.
